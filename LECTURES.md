* [You and Your Research](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=a1zDuOPkMSw) _By Richard Hamming (BellLabs, Turing Award)_
  * [Transcript](https://paulgraham.com/hamming.html) 

    Richard Hamming’s 1986 talk “You and Your Research” (Bellcore), on how to do outstanding work—not on managing others.

    Hamming argues that “great” science is not pure luck: repeated success follows a **prepared mind**, **courage**, and sustained drive (knowledge and productivity compound like interest). You should work on **important problems** you can actually attack (not just famous wish-list items), keep a lovely balance of belief and doubt, stay committed so your subconscious can work, and **communicate** (writing and talks) so the work is noticed. He adds practical color: “great thoughts” time, **open doors** vs. isolation, turning constraints into advantages, selling ideas without apology, and avoiding traps like fighting the system forever, ego that blocks collaboration, or early fame that stops you from planting new “acorns.” The end Q&A touches stress, management, reading vs. thinking, and changing fields periodically to stay fresh.

    1. Don't pick safe problems, tackle the important ones (even if risky)
    2. Keep doors open, let ideas and interruptions flow
    3. Use inversion, flip obstacles into advantages
    4. Compounding works, small daily edges multiply massively
